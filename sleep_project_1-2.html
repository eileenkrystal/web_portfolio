Do Certain Factors Contribute to Better/Worse Sleep Quality?¶

Eileen Vincent Bose

December 03, 2023

Background and Motivation¶

The importance of getting a good night's rest often goes unnoticed in the fast-paced world we live in today where technological advancements and career driven demands continue to rise. Sleep is a fundamental aspect of human health, playing an essential role in the physical, mental, and emotional well-being of a human. For this project, I wanted to see if there were any factors that impact sleep quality. More specifically, I wanted to see whether certain factors such as the daily steps a person takes, stress level, or occupation have a significant correlation with the quality of sleep. The motivation for this topic lies behind the amouont of sleep I have gotten over the past few months at night on an average. I was curious to see whether certain factors contribute to better/worse sleep quality.

From the daily steps vs. quality of sleep plot shown above, there is a positive correlation from the amount of daily steps a person takes in a day to their quality of sleep on a scale from 1-10 (1 being the worst and 10 being the best). Discussion and Conclusion¶

This dataset provided key insights into the various factors that play a role in the quality of sleep an individual receives. The three factors that I explored in my project were daily steps vs. quality of sleep, stress level vs. quality of sleep, and the average quality of sleep for accountants, doctors, nurses, engineers, teachers, and salespeople. In the daily steps vs quality of sleep plot, there was a positive correlation, indicating that individuals who took more daily steps tend to have a better quality of sleep. For a man that had around 3,000 daily steps, they rated their quality of sleep as a 4. A man that had around 8000 steps, rated their quality of sleep as 8.0. This trend was consistent for both men and women showing that the more physical activity a person has, the better the quality of sleep is.

The stress level vs. quality of sleep plot depicted a clear negative correlation for both men and women. This aligns with previous research that suggests high stress levels can negatively impact sleep quality. These results confirm the significance of stress management in order to gain an increased quality of sleep.

To find which occupation tends to get a higher quality of sleep, I found that accountants and engineers, on average, get the highest quality of sleep while nurses and salespersons had the lowest. Engineers had an average of 7.6 in their quality of sleep, while nurses had an average rating of 6.0. This makes sense as nurses also tend to work long hours in a single day, and a lot of their work includes walking on the hospital floor, getting to patient’s rooms, etc. This may lead to less amounts of sleep, thus poor quality of sleep.

Some of the obstacles I ran into were how I was going to get an equal amount of data values for both men and women, as well as the different occupations. There is a different sample size for the varying occupations, so I wanted to make sure that I filter out the data to get the same sample size for each occupation. Something I would do differently next time is to explore how different age groups and bmi levels impact the relationship between daily steps/stress levels and the quality of sleep. Overall, this project allowed me to get a better understanding of how to mask/clean data, look for trends, and plot these relationships. I was able to confirm my hypothesis that higher daily steps and low stress levels lead to a higher quality of sleep.
